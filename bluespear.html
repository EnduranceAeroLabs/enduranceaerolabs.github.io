§ 01 · Overview

Mission summary.

What we're building and why it matters.

BlueSpear is AIAA at UTA's student-led space launch vehicle program. The objective is to design, build, and fly a launch vehicle as a student-driven engineering exercise — with the kind of subsystem rigor and documentation discipline that production aerospace programs run on.

I came onto the program as Avionics Lead in February 2026, where I run a five-person avionics and command-and-data-handling team. The avionics subsystem owns the flight computer, sensor stack, power-rail definitions to the comms and recovery subsystems, and the interface to ground.

§ 02 · Objectives

Subsystem objectives.

Top-level avionics goals derived from mission intent.

  • OBJ-01 · Deliver a flight-computer architecture capable of running data acquisition, recovery logic, and telemetry within the vehicle's mass, power, and form-factor budgets.
  • OBJ-02 · Define sensor coverage — IMU, barometric altitude, GPS — sufficient to characterize trajectory, detect apogee, and validate recovery deployment.
  • OBJ-03 · Author interface control documents (ICDs) for every power and signal interface between avionics, comms, recovery, and propulsion.
  • OBJ-04 · Build a hazard / risk register tied to specific failure modes (e.g., premature deployment, brown-out, telemetry loss) with mitigations traceable to design choices.
  • OBJ-05 · Plan integration & test campaign — bench checkouts, end-to-end simulated flights, and pre-flight functional tests — so launch day is a verification step, not a debug session.

§ 06 · My Role

What I own.

Specific responsibilities and contributions.

As Avionics Lead, I own the subsystem end-to-end — from architecture down to the integration plan that hands the box off to the launch team. My day-to-day:

  • Architecture & design. Drive the avionics block diagram, sensor selection, and interface definitions. Make sure every signal and every rail has a documented owner and a budget.
  • Trade studies. Lead the team through structured trade studies on flight computer, IMU, GPS, and barometric candidates. Decisions tie back to weighted criteria, not vibes.
  • Documentation. Author the avionics ICDs, the risk register, and the integration-and-test plan. The documents are the deliverable — they're what the rest of the team builds against.
  • Team coordination. Run the avionics standup, unblock the five engineers, and represent avionics at program-level reviews.
  • Cross-subsystem interfaces. Sync with comms, recovery, propulsion, and structures leads on every interface that crosses subsystem boundaries.
